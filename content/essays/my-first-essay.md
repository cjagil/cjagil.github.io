I have been reading Ernst Cassier and his Renaissance Philosphers.  In the first instance, I am shocked that nobody ascribes the philosopher label to so many of these thinkers.  Giordano Bruno, Nicholas Cusanus, Pico, Paracelcus.  One is much more likely to hear of them called "occultists" or "hermeticists."  This is an apt label, but also maligning.  They are thinkers.  I see a lot of similarities between them and the pre-socratics.  Struggling from a priomordial soup[^soup] to bring reason and thought to bear on the world around them.  In a direct way its like watching people actually leave the cave for the first time.  The focus on relativity was particularly striking.

Here is a list of some of the quotes I caputured from the first and second chapters:

- Paraphrase: the divine is only revealed from the combination of every suboordinated, multiplicative viewpoint.  God is therefore the thing that is the sum of what every part of the universe, and therefore god, believes.
- Paraphrase: knowledge is always a specific direction of mental activity.
- "Time is the organ the soul uses to fulfill its basic function, which is to order and sift the multiplicity [of the universe]."
- Paraphrase: just as the eye never fills up from seeing, the soul never fills up from thinking and experiencing.
- "Cusansus considers man to be the bond that joins the world . . . . Because he is the representation of the universe and the essence of all its powers.  Man cannot be raised to the divine withotue simultaneously raising the rest of the universe by virtue of an within the process of man's own asencion."
- "The mind in its progress recognizes no 'ne plus ultra,' no limit to its triving."

Here is a list of some of the quotes I caputured from the third chapter, "Freedom and Necessity."

- "The being of man follows from his doing."
- "All true creativity implies more than mere action upon the world."
- "Freedom simply means that man does not recieve his being ready-made from nature . . . nor as a permanent fief; rather he must acquire it, must form it through *virtus and *ars."
- Paraphrase: Astrology presents a double intellectual front:
    (1) seeking to explain the movement of natural objects mathematically
    (2) ascribing phenomena to deomonology
- "Man does not recieve his being as something finished but forms it of his own free will."
- "Through the herioc passion that ignites wihtin him, man becomes equal to nature and able to comprehend its infinitiy and its incomensurability."

Another current I appreciated was the reference to Leonardo Da Vinci's "originators" vs "imitators" distinction.  The thoguht that an originator is the only true thinker struck a chord.  The mind free from sullied notions and restrictions is the most productive.  It resonates to me with the push towards unsupervised learning in AI.  Teaching a computer the strategy of chess is trying to code an imitator.  Teaching a computer the rules and saying figure it out is an originator.


[^soup]: The thing that lightning hit and created life.  Not Frankenstein's monster, however young.
