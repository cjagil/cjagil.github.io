I have kept notebooks for as long as I have distrusted my own memory, which is to
say for as long as I can remember.[^memory] The habit began as bookkeeping — a
ledger of things I was afraid to forget — and became, without my noticing, a kind
of thinking. The page does not merely store the thought; it changes it.

Montaigne, who is the patron saint of this little site, did not sit down to
*explain* anything. He sat down to find out what he made of a thing by writing
about it, and he was honest enough to let the writing wander. "I do not portray
being," he says somewhere; "I portray passing."[^passing] That is the whole
permission I need. A notebook entry is not a verdict. It is a person caught in the
act of changing their mind.

> The mind, once stretched by a new idea, never returns to its original
> dimensions.

What I have noticed is that the act of writing a thing down does two opposite
things at once. It *fixes* the thought — gives it edges, a beginning and an end,
a sentence it must live inside. And it *loosens* the thought, because now that it
exists outside my head I can turn it over, disagree with it, find it embarrassing
next year. A thought I have not written is safe from revision. That safety is
exactly the problem.

So this is the experiment of the site: to think slowly, in public, and to leave
the older drafts visible so that the changing is part of the record. If you catch
me contradicting an earlier page, that is not a bug. That is the notebook working.

[^memory]: A convenient thing to believe about oneself, since it excuses the
    notebook.

[^passing]: From the *Essais*, Book III. I am quoting from memory, which is to
    say I am quoting the notebook.
